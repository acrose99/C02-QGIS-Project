Map 1: Total Carbon Dioxide Emissions (In metric tons) per year, by Country.

Properties of Map:

  • Database:  UN statistics on carbon dioxide emissons
  • https://hub.arcgis.com/datasets/undesa::syb-indicator-carbon-dioxide-emissions?geometry=162.248%2C15.760%2C12.658%2C60.999&selectedAttribute=value_2014
  • Visuals: For each 100,000 metric tons of C02 emissions, the point- radius of the country increases by 20 millimeters. This functions as a scaled heatmap, the large and brighter the point, the more C02 is being emitted. The point-radius is than layered through a negative filter for visualization purposes. The point-radiuses of each country are than scaled, through a shapefile, to OpenStreetMap, which is a dynamic, open source map of the world.
  • Label/Statistics: Each country’s ISO code is concatenated with metric tons of carbon dioxide emissions per year (latest value, usually from 2014) . The label is filtered to only show countries that emit greater or equal to 200,000 metric tons of C02 emissions, to avoid map clutter.

Brief summary of findings:

Although this database serves as the ‘baseline’ for all my other maps, statistics, and findings in general, there are still some things that stick out immediately.

The most noticeable feature on the heatmap is how large the U.S’s, and particularly China’s point-radius of their C02 emissions is, compared to other respective countries. Statistically, this makes sense because they emit so much more C02 than other countries, the U.S emits 5254279.28metric tons of C02 per year and China emits 10291926.87metric tons of C02 per year. Compared to the median country, the Netherlands, who emits 5819.52 metric tons of C02 per year, these two Countries are significantly higher.

One of the less noticeable features on the heatmap are the countries I’ll call secondary emitters, countries who emit large amounts of C02, and emit a semi-large point-radius, but also emit significantly less than the U.S and China. For example, India (2238377.13 metric tons) and Russia (1705345.68 metric tons) emit enough C02 to be visible on the heatmap, but not enough to light up a large portion of the map like the U.S and China.

Another interesting feature is visible both in the Middle East and Western Europe. Because most countries in those respective regions are secondary emitters, like Germany and Iran, a solid strip of dull orange appears in these regions.

Finally, in regions without much C02 emissions, I.E Africa and South America, no point-radiuses are visible at all. If you zoom in slightly, Brazil and South Africa’s point-radius will appear, since they emit the most C02 of their respective continents (this is probably a bug, their data signifies they should have a slightly larger point-radius from this view). However, generally, these countries admit a small enough proportion of total C02 emissions that they don’t appear, because of the way the map is scaled.  

Moving forward, using other databases, we will try to find so correlative statistics to explain these phenomena.

Map 2: Total Carbon Dioxide Emissions (In metric tons) per capita, per year, by Country:

Properties of Map:

  • Database:  Model UN statistics on carbon dioxide emissions, per capita  
  • https://hub.arcgis.com/datasets/undesa::syb-indicator-carbon-dioxide-emissions-per-capita
  • Visuals: The point- radius of the country increases by 10 millimeters, and is auto scaled by the latest value of carbon dioxide emissions, per capita. This functions as a scaled heatmap, the large and brighter the point, the more C02 is being emitted, per capita. The point-radius is than layered through a negative filter for visualization purposes. The point-radiuses of each country are than scaled, through a shapefile, to OpenStreetMap, which is a dynamic, open source map of the world.
  • Label/Statistics: Each country’s ISO code is concatenated with metric tons of carbon dioxide emissions per year, per capita (latest value, usually from 2014) . The label is filtered to only show countries that emit greater or equal to 6.5 metric tons of C02 emissions, per capita, to avoid map clutter.

Brief summary of findings:

This map shows a correlation between total C02 emissions, development (which is inferred), and the population of countries (per capita). At first, C02 emissions per capita seems to be independent from Map 1. For example, despite being the country leader in C02 emissions, China only emits 7.5174 metrics tons of C02, when adjusted per capita. Similarly, the U.S emits 16.2448 metric tons of C02, when adjusted per capita, coming in 16th place, when sorted from most emissions to least.

Generally, Highly developed countries have the largest C02 emissions, per capita, and developing countries have the lowest C02 emissions, per capita. The difference in C02 emissions per capita is likely because higher developed countries have a higher consumer demand for goods and services that emit CO2. This is visible in the heatmap and labeling for Western Europe, and richer countries in the Middle East, like Qatar, which has the highest C02 emissions per capita at 49.6512 metric tons. Simillary, no heatmap is visible for most of Africa, which is generally less developed.

This tells us that generally, highly developed countries emit the most C02, when adjusted for population.  

In some of the next maps, this trend of highly developed countries emitting the most C02, when adjusted for population will continue.

Brief summary of findings:

The map and data of this database is strikingly similar to the first map (Total C02 emissions). The U.S and China once again light up the map because of how much more C02 they emit from fuel combustion than everyone else (9056.8 and 4833.081 millions of tons, respectively). Secondary emitters like India and Russia are also visible (2076.833 and 1438.598 millions of tons, respectively). A solid strip of orange appears in the middle east and western europe, again, because they are secondary emitters. Finally, countries without much fuel consumption also do not appear on the heatmap.

In fact, if we convert the fuel consumption to metric tons to match the first map, the emissions from fuel consumption usually makes up around 90% of the total fuel emissions, give or take around 10%.

Brief summary of findings:

There is a strong negative correlation between large rates, which I’ve defined as greater than 2%, of annual population growth, and a positive correlation between small and/or negative rates, which I’ve defined has  of annual population growth. Most countries with a large annual population growth, such as Angola (3.282) emit low levels of C02 (34763) metric tons. On the other hand, many countries with a low/negative annual population growth emit large levels of C02, like China whom has a annual population growth rate of .39% but emits 10291926.878000000491738 metric tons of C02 per year.

This tells us that generally, countries that have high annual population rate growth generally do not emit much C02, and that countries that have low/negative annual population growth may emit a lot of C02. This makes sense under the overall trend of highly developed countries emitting the most C02, because higher annual population rate growth is usually found in developing countries.

Part 2: Summary  

Explanation: Using the maps and findings of the maps below, I will summarize my overall findings of this project.  

When looking at a visualization of total C02 emissions, one can comprehend just how much Carbon Dioxide is emitted by China and the US. These two countries, combined, makeup almost half of the total C02 emissions across the world. In fact, they admit so much CO2 than most other countries' point-radius on the heatmap fail to appear since the point-radiuses are scaled. However, you can also see the existence of ‘secondary emitters, which are countries that emit a significant amount of C02, but significantly less than the U.S and China. Some of these countries are Russia and India, where their point-radius is easily visible but less so than the U.S and China. In compact, and highly developed regions like Western Europe and the Middle East (which is boosted by oil production), the emissions of most countries are enough to be considered secondary emitters, so point-radiuses of countries within these regions form an orange stripe.

   However, using the same visualization, the regions of South America, Oceania, and Africa have barely visible point-radiuses. This is because none of the countries in these regions (with the exception of Brazil and South Africa, which should barely appear but just barely miss the scale I auto-generated, still they only account for around 1% of total emissions) emit a significant amount of the world’s C02.

We use these findings, that China and the U.S emit approximately 50% of all C02 emissions, as a baseline for all of our other findings. Most of these other findings can be generalized into the fact the higher developed countries emit more C02 than less-developed countries.

When looking at indicators of development of different countries, in our case, the rate of annual population growth, the proportion of population below the poverty rate, this becomes more obvious. The visual contrast between countries with higher population growth and countries with a high proportion of the population below the poverty rate, compared to the total C02 emissions is stark. The heatmap for the countries with higher population growth and countries with a high proportion of the population below the poverty rate is strongest in Africa, and to a lesser extent South America, while the heatmap for total C02 emissions in these areas is practically non-existent.  

The trend is reinforced, when looking at a visualization of total C02 emissions, per capita. Countries that have higher development (I.E those who do not appear in the heatmap in the previous two examples) emit far more C02, per capita the countries with low development. However, the total C02 emissions per capita put a small dent into our theory, because the U.S and China, the two largest emitters by far, have relatively small C02 emissions per capita.

However, the C02 emissions per capita put into question why China and the U.S are the largest C02 emitters, since their C02 emissions per capita are relatively low, which is visualized on the heatmap.

Therefore, we use the amount of C02 emissions from fuel combustion to explain this peculiarity. Both China and the U.S are by far the largest consumers of fuel, because of the energy demand for both countries (which is beyond the scope of this project) which burns a significant amount of C02. Specifically, the U.S likely consumes so much fuel because of consumer demand caused by its wealth, and China because of consumer demand caused by its large population. This is visualized by the respective heatmap for C02 emissions from fuel combustion, which is similar to the heat map for total C02 emissions.

As an aside, using a visualization of the number of motor vehicles in use per country, you can see that the common conception that the amount of cars used is an indicator for the number of carbon emissions burned, is a false assumption.

Finally, we can say with certainty, that China and the U.S are the largest emitters of C02 because of the amount of fuel they consume, and that highly developed countries emit more C02 than lower developed countries.
